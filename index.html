Featured Project

SEAL Team Project: Adaptive Management Plan for Seals near Churchill, Manitoba

Project Overview

This project was completed as part of the Students Engaged in Applied Learning (SEAL) Team at the University of Manitoba. The full team included Paige A. Newcomb, Emma R. Burton, Conner R. O’Dowda, and Dev K. Dave. The final project was designed as a collaborative wildlife management plan, and I want to give equal credit to the full team for the research, planning, discussion, writing, and final development of the report.

Our project focused on bearded seals, ringed seals, and harbour seals near Churchill, Manitoba. We developed an adaptive management plan in response to major ecological and human-related pressures affecting these species, especially climate change, increased tourism, and growing shipping activity associated with the Port of Churchill.

The purpose of the plan was not only to study seal populations, but also to create a practical framework for long-term monitoring, conservation decision-making, and management actions that could reduce harm while improving understanding of seal population stability in western Hudson Bay.

Why This Project Was Needed

The project recognized that seals are ecologically important but often receive less attention than larger Arctic species. Our report highlighted how sea ice loss, disturbance from tourism, and shipping-related noise and habitat disruption can affect seal behaviour, feeding, pupping, moulting, and long-term resilience.

Team-Based Approach

This project brought together wildlife science, environmental research, management planning, mapping, policy awareness, stakeholder thinking, and presentation development. It was built through shared team effort, multiple meetings, expert feedback, and step-by-step revision through the semester.

Core Objectives

Our plan had two major goals. The first was to support long-term population stability through baseline population assessment and repeated monitoring. The second was to reduce anthropogenic harm by improving shipping practices and encouraging conservation-focused tourism practices in the Churchill region.

Research and Monitoring Design

The project proposed initial population research followed by a five-year monitoring cycle. For bearded and ringed seals, the plan recommended aerial visual direct count surveys with photographic support. For harbour seals, the plan proposed land-based visual direct count surveys supported by existing telemetry data and local observations.

We also emphasized that repeated monitoring should stay flexible and adaptive. If better methods become available in the future, such as drone-based approaches or other advanced techniques, the plan allows for modification while maintaining scientific consistency.

Study Area and Indigenous Involvement

The selected observation area extended from the Seal River in the north to Fort Churchill in the south and included both marine and freshwater environments. This area was chosen because it captured important habitat features for all three seal species while also overlapping with places affected by tourism and port activity.

A major strength of the project was the recognition that Indigenous involvement must be central to planning and implementation. The report specifically called for collaboration that respects traditional ecological knowledge, local values, and equitable participation.

Shipping Management Recommendations

One of the most detailed parts of the project focused on shipping impacts. The plan recommended buffer zones and speed reductions near key seal-use areas and ports, especially within the project area surrounding Churchill. The goal was to reduce disturbance from vessel noise, ice breakage, and other shipping-related effects.

The project also proposed using AIS-based shipping data to track vessel activity and help support future management and enforcement.

Tourism and Ecotourism Recommendations

Our report also addressed seal disturbance caused by tourism. We recommended more conservation-focused ecotourism practices, greater viewing distance, careful use of vehicles and boats, and stronger attention to seasons when seals are especially vulnerable, such as pupping and haul-out periods.

The project argued that wildlife viewing should be designed around minimizing stress and behavioural disruption rather than maximizing close contact.

Budget and Phased Implementation

The report proposed an estimated budget of about $2 million CAD, including aerial survey costs, data analysis, community-based monitoring, and shipping-related technology. It recommended a two-phase rollout: Phase 1 focused on data collection, baseline assessment, and early regulations, while Phase 2 focused on stronger long-term management actions based on monitoring results.
